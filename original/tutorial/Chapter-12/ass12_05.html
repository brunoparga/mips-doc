No-Op

Register $0 always contains a 32-bit zero so shifting it left by zero positions and attempting to put the result back in $0 does nothing. Any instruction that attempts to alter $0 does nothing, but this instruction is the preferred way of doing nothing.

A machine instruction that does nothing is called (in official computer science jargon) a no-op. The no operation instruction is surprisingly useful, especially for MIPS.
